# ORBWEVA Pitch Night

Build and rehearse a short spoken pitch that lands with judges who do **not** know your home market. Built for ORBWEVA Accelerator participants pitching at demo nights and competitions (e.g. the World-Ready Pitch Night: 3-minute pitch, 4-minute warning, 5-minute hard cut, judged on cross-cultural clarity).

This is not an investor deck. It is the 3 minutes you say out loud. The failure mode it fixes: a founder who is fluent in their own market loses a foreign judge in the first 30 seconds because the judge cannot picture the customer, the problem, or how big it is.

## The judging rubric (what they actually score)

World-Ready judges score whether the pitch communicates to people unfamiliar with the founder's local market. Four dimensions — write the pitch to hit each one on purpose:

1. **Cross-cultural clarity** — can a judge in another country picture the customer and the problem without local knowledge? This is the dimension most pitches lose.
2. **Positioning** — what it is, who it's for, why it's different, in one breath.
3. **Go-to-market** — how the first 10 / next 100 customers are reached. Concrete channel, not "social media".
4. **Market size** — how big this gets, grounded in a number a foreigner can sanity-check, not a top-down "1% of a trillion-dollar market".

A pitch that nails 1–3 and waves at 4 beats a pitch that buries all four in jargon.

## Stage 1: Build the 3-minute script (`/pitch-night:build`)

Three minutes is ~390–450 spoken words at a calm pace. Budget it. Don't write 600 and rush.

**The structure (second budget for a 3-min slot):**

| Beat | Time | Say |
|---|---|---|
| Hook + who you are | 0:00–0:20 | One concrete sentence a stranger pictures instantly. Name + venture + the one thing it does. |
| The problem, made foreign-legible | 0:20–0:50 | Who has the problem, in terms that travel. One real person, one real moment. (See Stage 2.) |
| The product / how it works | 0:50–1:30 | What you built, shown not described. One sentence of "before → after". |
| Traction / proof | 1:30–2:00 | The most concrete evidence you have: paying customers, revenue, interviews, a waitlist, a built thing. A real number beats an adjective. |
| Go-to-market | 2:00–2:30 | The specific channel for the first customers, and why it works for *this* buyer. |
| Market size + the ask | 2:30–3:00 | How big it gets (bottom-up), then the single ask (intro, pilot, capital, users). Close on the ask, not on "thank you". |

**Rules:**
- One claim per sentence. Plain verbs. Active voice.
- Lead with the outcome, not the origin story. The "why I built this" goes in *one* line if at all.
- Replace every adjective with a number or a proper noun. "Lots of users" → "320 on the waitlist". "A big market" → "11,000 X businesses in Y".
- Cut every word the judge already knows. No "in today's world", no "revolutionary", no "leverage / unlock / seamless".

## Stage 2: Make your market legible (`/pitch-night:translate`)

This is the dimension foreign-market founders lose, and the one ORBWEVA founders should win. The move: **translate one layer of local context per claim** so a judge with zero local knowledge can still follow.

For each of customer, problem, channel, and competitor, run it through:

- **Anchor to a known reference.** "It's the Japanese equivalent of [thing the judge knows]." Bridge the unknown to the known in five words.
- **Convert local units.** Currency to the judge's currency or USD; local platform names get one clause of explanation ("LINE — Japan's dominant messaging app, ~95M users"); regulations get the one-line "why it matters".
- **Show the customer as a person, not a segment.** "Aiko, a 34-year-old salon owner in Osaka" beats "SMB service providers in the APAC region". A foreign judge can hold a person; they can't hold a segment.
- **State what's true *only* in your market** — and turn it into the edge. The local wrinkle a foreign competitor would miss is usually the moat. Name it.

If the pitch is for an Australia × Japan audience specifically:
- **AU founder, JP judges (or vice-versa):** don't assume shared SME context, banking rails, or platform defaults. Spell out the one structural fact (e.g. cash-heavy economy, hanko/seal culture, keigo, GST vs consumption tax) that changes the product.
- **Either direction:** the cross-border angle is itself a selling point at a World-Ready night — say explicitly why this travels (or why winning one market is the wedge into the other).

## Stage 3: Rehearse against the clock (`/pitch-night:rehearse`)

- **Time it out loud, three times, with a real timer.** Page-time lies; mouth-time is truth. If it runs to the 4-minute warning, you're 25% over — cut, don't speed up.
- **Cut to land 10 seconds early.** Judges read a pitch that finishes inside the limit as in-control; one that gets hard-cut at 5:00 reads as not ready. End on the ask with breathing room.
- **The first 20 seconds carry the room.** Rehearse the hook until it's automatic so nerves don't eat it.
- **Have a 90-second version ready.** Q&A and overruns happen; know what survives if you lose half your time. (Hook → problem → proof → ask.)
- **Remove filler and hedges** — "kind of", "I think", "it's worth noting". Each one costs clarity and seconds.
- **Screen-share / demo:** assume it breaks. Have one screenshot and one sentence that carry the product if the live demo dies. Never spend pitch-time debugging.
- **Speak for the mic, not the room** — it's virtual (Google Meet). Look at the camera, not the gallery.

## Stage 4: Self-score (`/pitch-night:score`)

Score the draft 1–5 on each, then fix the lowest two before rehearsing:

| Dimension | 1 (lose) | 5 (win) |
|---|---|---|
| Cross-cultural clarity | Judge needs local knowledge to follow | A stranger pictures the customer and problem in 30s |
| Positioning | "It's a platform for…" mush | What/who/why-different in one breath |
| Go-to-market | "Marketing and social media" | One named channel + why it fits this buyer |
| Market size | Top-down % of a huge number | Bottom-up count a foreigner can sanity-check |
| Time discipline | Hard-cut at the limit | Lands 10s early on the ask |
| Proof | Adjectives | A real number or a built thing |

Total ≥ 26 / 30 is pitch-ready. Below that, the lowest dimension is the rewrite target.
